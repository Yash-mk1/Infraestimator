Studies, inspiration & thinking

InfraMonitor was built on a body of existing research in structural health monitoring, computer vision, and deep learning for crack detection. Below are the studies, datasets, and ideas that shaped our approach.

Research that inspired us

Dataset 2019

CrackForest Dataset

A benchmark dataset of 118 road pavement images with pixel-level crack annotations. One of the first publicly available datasets for crack segmentation, it established the evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F-measure) used in most subsequent crack detection research. Our OpenCV pipeline's threshold choices were informed by how models perform on this dataset.

Shi et al. · IEEE Transactions on Circuits and Systems for Video Technology
Dataset 2018

AigleRN Dataset

A road surface crack dataset collected from French roads, containing images under various lighting conditions including shadows and wet surfaces. This dataset directly informed our shadow rejection logic — many false positives in early testing came from road shadows that looked identical to longitudinal cracks under Canny edge detection.

Chambon & Moliard · IEEE Transactions on Intelligent Transportation Systems
Architecture 2023

YOLOv8 / YOLOv11 for Instance Segmentation

Ultralytics' YOLO family extended object detection into pixel-level instance segmentation — the ability to output a mask for each detected object rather than a bounding box. This was the key architectural decision that made our approach possible: instead of knowing a crack exists somewhere in a rectangle, we know exactly which pixels are crack. The nano variant (yolo11n-seg) was chosen for its ability to run on CPU without a GPU.

Jocher et al. · Ultralytics · ultralytics.com
Method 1986

Canny Edge Detection

John Canny's edge detector remains one of the most effective methods for finding crack-like features in images. Its two-threshold hysteresis approach — keeping strong edges and weak edges connected to strong ones — makes it ideal for tracing the thin, continuous lines of structural cracks. We use it as the backbone of our OpenCV fallback pipeline, enhanced with CLAHE preprocessing to handle uneven lighting.

Canny, J. · IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence
Method 1994

Morphological Skeletonisation

Thinning binary crack masks to single-pixel-wide skeletons preserves the topology (connectivity and branching) of a crack network while removing thickness variation caused by image resolution or lighting. This is critical for measuring crack length and filtering short noise segments — a skeleton segment shorter than 15 pixels is almost certainly not a structural crack.

Zhang & Suen · Communications of the ACM · implemented in scikit-image
Preprocessing 1994

CLAHE — Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation

Standard histogram equalisation can over-amplify noise in uniform regions. CLAHE divides the image into tiles and equalises each locally, with a clip limit to prevent noise amplification. For infrastructure images — which often have overexposed concrete and underexposed crack interiors — CLAHE significantly improves crack visibility before edge detection runs.

Zuiderveld · Graphics Gems IV · implemented in OpenCV

Our design decisions

Why 60% AI + 40% OpenCV?

A trained model is more accurate on crack types it has seen during training, but can miss unusual crack patterns or fail on low-quality images. OpenCV is consistent but generates more false positives. By merging both with AI at higher weight, we get the accuracy of the model where it's confident, and the coverage of rule-based detection where it isn't.

Why not use just the AI model?

Our crack model was trained on 26 epochs of a 4,000-image dataset — a solid start but not enough to generalise perfectly to every surface type, lighting condition, and camera angle. The OpenCV layer acts as a safety net. When the model is retrained on more data, the OpenCV weight can be reduced.

Why score spread, not just area?

A close-up photo of a single crack has high pixel coverage but low structural risk compared to the same number of pixels spread across an entire wall. Measuring the bounding box of the crack network relative to the image size gives a better proxy for how widespread the damage actually is.

Why a score floor of 1.0?

A structure that is being photographed and scanned is still standing. Reporting 0.0 years remaining life implies total collapse, which is never literally the case at the time of inspection. The floor of 1.0 and minimum lifetime of 1 year reflects that even severely damaged structures have some residual service life before intervention.
