// How it works

From photo to health report

InfraMonitor combines a trained AI segmentation model with classical computer vision to detect structural damage — then converts those findings into a single health score and lifetime estimate.

01

Image input

You upload a photo or capture one via your device camera. The image is sent to the Django backend where it is decoded, validated, and resized to a maximum of 1600px on the longest side to keep inference time consistent.

Accepted formats: JPG · PNG · WEBP · BMP
02

Wall isolation

Before any damage is measured, the system identifies the dominant flat surface in the image — the wall, floor, or structural element being inspected. Regions with high edge density (door frames, pipes, furniture) are excluded from analysis so that non-structural objects don't inflate the damage score.

Algorithm: edge density mapping + largest flat-region extraction
03

Shadow rejection

Real-world photos contain shadows that look like cracks to edge detectors. The system identifies shadow regions by checking for low brightness, low saturation, and smooth gradients — properties that distinguish shadows from the sharp, dark edges of actual cracks. Shadow pixels are excluded from damage analysis.

Algorithm: HSV shadow model + Laplacian gradient sharpness check
04

AI crack detection — 60%

A YOLOv11 instance segmentation model, trained on the Surface Crack Segmentation dataset (4,000+ images), runs inference on the image. It outputs a pixel-level mask marking regions it predicts are cracks. This mask contributes 60% of the final combined crack mask.

Model: YOLOv11n-seg · Dataset: Surface Crack Segmentation · mAP@50: 78.7%
05

OpenCV crack detection — 40%

Alongside the AI model, a classical computer vision pipeline runs independently. CLAHE contrast enhancement sharpens the image, Canny edge detection finds crack edges, and morphological skeletonisation thins them to single-pixel lines. This mask contributes 40% of the final combined crack mask — ensuring cracks the AI misses are still caught.

Algorithm: CLAHE → Canny → Dilation → Skeletonisation → Length filter
06

Seep & moisture detection

A second model handles seepage, moisture staining, and rust. Where a seep model is available it runs at 60% weight alongside an OpenCV colour-space analysis (HSV channel thresholds for dark-wet regions, rust hues, and blue-green moisture deposits) at 40% weight. Seep pixels that overlap with crack regions are removed to avoid double-counting.

Colour ranges: rust (H:5–25) · moisture (H:85–135) · dark-wet (V<85, S>45)
07

False positive filtering

Every detected blob is tested for crack-like geometry before it is counted. Blobs that are too round (circularity > 0.65) or too square (aspect ratio < 1.8) are removed — cracks are elongated and thin, not circular or blobby.

Filters: circularity · bounding-box aspect ratio · minimum area (60 px²)
08

Surface degradation scoring

Independent of crack and seep detection, the wall region is analysed for general surface degradation — spalling, erosion, and texture breakdown. Local variance maps and Laplacian sharpness measure how much the surface has deteriorated beyond visible cracking.

Algorithm: Local variance map + Laplacian sharpness score
09

Health score calculation

Three sub-scores are combined into one damage index. Crack severity carries 50% weight (factoring in spread across the surface and number of separate regions), seep/moisture carries 25%, and surface degradation carries 25%. The damage index is subtracted from 10, floored at 1.0, and rounded to one decimal place.

Score = 10 − (crack×0.50 + seep×0.25 + surface×0.25) − penalties
10

Lifetime estimation

Remaining service life is estimated from the health score using material-specific baseline lifetimes. Above a score of 4.0 a power-law curve is used; below 4.0 a linear model prevents the estimate collapsing to zero for heavily damaged but still-standing structures. The minimum reported lifetime is 1 year.

Baselines: Concrete 80yr · Brick 100yr · Steel 60yr · Wood 40yr · Asphalt 25yr
